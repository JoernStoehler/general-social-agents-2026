# Reasoning Guide: Predicting Human Behavior in Games

When predicting human behavior in economic games, you should draw on and expand upon the following concepts. These are starting points — recall everything you know about each, including related and opposing ideas, and apply or skip as your judgment suggests.

## Bounded rationality
Humans have limited cognitive resources and time. They do not solve games optimally. They use heuristics, rules of thumb, and limited-depth reasoning. Herbert Simon's "satisficing" — people find good-enough solutions, not optimal ones.

## Level-k reasoning
In strategic games, level-0 players choose naively (e.g., randomly or salient option). Level-1 best-responds to level-0. Level-2 best-responds to level-1. Empirically, most humans reason at level 1-3, with the modal level around 2. This is well-established across many experimental games (Nagel 1995, Stahl & Wilson 1994, Costa-Gomes et al. 2001).

## Social preferences
Humans are not purely self-interested. They care about:
- **Fairness / inequality aversion** (Fehr & Schmidt 1999, Bolton & Ockenfels 2000): dislike unequal outcomes, especially disadvantageous inequality
- **Reciprocity** (Rabin 1993, Charness & Rabin 2002): reward kind intentions, punish hostile ones
- **Efficiency** (Charness & Rabin 2002): prefer higher total surplus, all else equal
- **Spite / competitive preferences**: some fraction of people prefer relative advantage

## Focal points and salience
Schelling's focal points: when multiple equilibria exist, people coordinate on salient options (round numbers, symmetric outcomes, culturally prominent choices).

## Risk attitudes and loss aversion
Humans are generally risk-averse over gains, risk-seeking over losses (Kahneman & Tversky 1979). Small probabilities are overweighted. Large probabilities are underweighted. The stakes matter — larger stakes increase risk aversion.

## Anchoring
People's judgments are influenced by salient numbers in the problem. In a game with range 11-20, both endpoints are anchors. In allocation games, equal splits are anchors.

## Distribution shape
Human choice distributions in experiments are typically peaked, not uniform. People cluster around a few focal strategies. The distribution often has a single mode with moderate spread, unless the game has multiple distinct "types" of reasoning that lead to different actions.

## What NOT to do
- Do not predict the Nash equilibrium distribution unless you believe the specific population would play Nash
- Do not hedge toward 50-50 or uniform out of uncertainty — commit to your best model of human behavior
- Do not apply one generic heuristic to all games — reason about each game's specific structure

# Task: Predict human choices in 20 allocation games

## The experiments

These are 20 two-stage dictator games from Charness & Rabin (2002, Quarterly Journal of Economics). Human data from university students playing for real money.

**Game structure**: In each game:
- **Person A** chooses between "Out" (a fixed allocation) or "Enter" (lets Person B decide)
- **Person B** chooses between "Left" and "Right" (two different allocations)
- Person B chooses WITHOUT knowing what A chose (strategy method)
- Both players know all payoffs in advance

**Important**: Because B chooses without knowing A's decision (strategy method), B's choice reflects their unconditional preference between Left and Right. This is not a strategic response to A — it's a direct preference revelation.

**Key behavioral factors in these games**:
- **Self-interest**: People prefer more money for themselves, all else equal
- **Inequality aversion**: People dislike unfair outcomes, especially when they get less
- **Efficiency**: People often prefer the allocation with higher total payoff
- **Reciprocity**: B may "reward" or "punish" A by choosing allocations that help/hurt A, even at cost to self. But remember: B doesn't know A's choice, so reciprocity here is about B's general attitude toward the game structure, not a response to A's actual action
- **When multiple motives conflict**: Different people weigh these differently. Some are mostly selfish, some are fairness-oriented, some are efficiency-oriented. The DISTRIBUTION reflects this mixture of types in the population
- **Extreme predictions are common**: In games where one option clearly dominates on multiple dimensions (self-interest + fairness + efficiency all point the same way), 80-95% of people may choose it. Do NOT default to moderate predictions — many of these games have a clearly dominant option for most people

## How to approach this

Work through each game individually. For each game:
1. Identify what motives (self-interest, fairness, efficiency, reciprocity) point which direction
2. Consider whether the motives align (→ extreme prediction) or conflict (→ moderate prediction)
3. Commit to a specific prediction — don't default to 0.50 or 0.60 out of uncertainty

Many games have one option that dominates on multiple dimensions. When that happens, predict 80-95% choosing it. Only predict moderate splits when motives genuinely conflict.
